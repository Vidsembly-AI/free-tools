WAV and MP3 are two of the most common audio formats, but they serve very different purposes. Choosing the wrong one can mean bloated file sizes, lost quality, or compatibility headaches downstream.

## What is WAV?

WAV (Waveform Audio File Format) stores **uncompressed** audio. Every sample of the recording is preserved exactly as captured. That makes WAV ideal when:

- You are **editing or mastering** audio and need maximum fidelity
- You are archiving source recordings you may re-process later
- You are working in a professional audio pipeline (DAW, broadcast, post-production)

The downside is file size. A stereo WAV at 44.1 kHz can be roughly **10 MB per minute** — fine for short clips, but impractical for long recordings or email attachments.

## What is MP3?

MP3 uses **lossy compression** to discard audio data the human ear is unlikely to notice. The result is a much smaller file — often **1 MB per minute or less** at 128 kbps — with quality that is perfectly acceptable for:

- Podcasts, voiceovers, and lecture recordings
- Background music in videos and presentations
- Sharing files via email, Slack, or cloud storage
- Web playback where bandwidth matters

Once you convert to MP3, you cannot recover the discarded data. Always keep your original WAV if you might need to re-edit or re-export at a higher quality later.

## Quick decision guide

| Scenario | Recommended format |
| --- | --- |
| Recording in a studio or DAW | WAV |
| Sharing a podcast episode | MP3 |
| Archiving a master recording | WAV |
| Embedding audio in a slide deck | MP3 |
| Uploading to a learning platform | MP3 |
| Sending a rough cut for review | MP3 |
